---
title: Best practices
description: Small habits that make a Hackbox game feel smooth and responsive.
---

Hackbox gives you a blank canvas on every player's phone. These habits keep that canvas feeling alive and trustworthy.

## Always send a complete screen

Every `member.update` [replaces a player's whole state](/basics/concepts#state-is-a-full-replacement) — it's merged onto blank defaults, not onto what they're currently seeing. Build each screen as a complete object (theme, header, components) rather than trying to "patch" the last one. [Presets](/advanced/presets) and a shared theme keep that from getting repetitive.

## Welcome your players

When a player joins, Hackbox shows a plain default "Hang tight!" screen — on purpose. Replace it. Sending an on-brand welcome the moment they appear in `state.host` confirms they're in the right place and sets the tone for your game.

## Confirm every submission

When a player submits, Hackbox disables the relevant control so they can't double-send — but silence still feels broken. Push a fresh screen acknowledging the action. Prefer specific confirmations over generic ones:

- ❌ Answer submitted.
- ✅ You answered **42**. Stay tuned for the result!
- ❌ Answer rejected.
- ✅ Your answer is too high — pick a number between 1 and 100.

## Keep players updated

The more often you update a player's screen, the more connected and engaged they feel. Compare two trivia flows:

**Too quiet**

1. Player gets a question.
2. Player answers.
3. Repeat.

**Engaging**

1. Player gets a question.
2. Player answers.
3. Confirm the answer was received.
4. Reveal whether it was right, and show their new score.
5. Tell them to get ready for the next question.
6. Repeat.

The second keeps something happening on screen at all times. The [trivia walkthrough](/tutorials/trivia-walkthrough) implements exactly this loop.

## Use `change` for timed rounds

Inputs emit a [`change`](/basics/concepts#events-from-players) event as the player edits, before they submit. Keep the latest `change` value per player and you can accept their in-progress selection when a timer expires — no one loses an answer just because they didn't tap submit in time.

## Lean on `ms` for fairness and flair

Every `msg`/`change` includes `ms` — the time from the component appearing to the interaction. Use it for speed bonuses, "first to buzz" tiebreakers, or just to show off how fast someone answered.

## Design for thumbs

Players are on phones, one-handed. Make tap targets big, keep the number of components per screen small, and use `ui.main.align: "end"` to drop primary buttons near the bottom where thumbs rest. A `Buzzer` or full-width `Button` beats a row of tiny controls.

## Reconnections take care of themselves

If a player's phone drops and rejoins, Hackbox [replays their last screen](/advanced/errors-and-lifecycle#reconnecting-and-state-replay) automatically — you don't need to detect reconnects or re-push UI. Just keep your own game state authoritative on the host, and the player picks up where they left off.
